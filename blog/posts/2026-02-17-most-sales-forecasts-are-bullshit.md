<div><p>Most sales forecasts are bullshit. Even at the billion-dollar level. Mattel just proved it.</p><p>Mattel's stock dropped 31% in one day. They reported $1.76B in Q4 revenue, up 7% year over year, BUT still managed to destroy 31% of their market value in a single day. Operating profit dropped 11%, and the CEO immediately pointed at tariffs and “retailer uncertainty.</p><p>”This didn’t happen because of ' tariffs' or 'uncertainty.” Tariffs weren’t new. Retailers delaying orders wasn’t new. All of this was visible months earlier. What failed was the forecast. </p><p>Management treated the retailer's “interest” as demand. They ramped up shipments in October and November, assuming Q4 would behave like it always does. That wasn’t forecasting. That was hope dressed up as a spreadsheet.</p><p>When the orders didn’t come in on time, they did what hope-based systems always do under pressure: they discounted heavily to clear inventory. More units moved. Margins collapsed. Profit disappeared. </p><p>Discounting didn’t fix the problem. It exposed it. </p><p>This isn’t a macro story. It’s a sequencing failure. Soft signals were treated as commitments. A green pipeline was treated like cash. Inventory was shipped without certainty. I see the same pattern every week in smaller companies, just with fewer zeros. Founders build forecasts based on what customers say rather than what they’ve committed to, because pushing for real commitment feels uncomfortable. Then they blame the market.</p><p>Mattel didn’t get punished for tariffs. They got punished for believing their own forecast and using discounts as a band-aid. </p><p>Revenue without certainty is noise. Hope is not a strategy .Discounting is not a solution.</p><p>“Unexpected” is just the bill coming due.</p><p>P.S. When I step in as a fractional CRO, the first thing I kill is forecast fiction. If your revenue plan depends on “interest” instead of commitment, your numbers are already lying to you.</p></div>
