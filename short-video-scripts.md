# Short-Form Video Script Pack - Tal Paperin

Goal: turn the hot takes already on talpaperin.com into 30 to 60 second videos for LinkedIn,
TikTok, Instagram Reels and YouTube Shorts. The job of every clip is the same as the site:
a B2B founder or revenue leader sees it, recognizes the pain, and ends up on the site or in
your DMs. We are not selling in the video. We are proving you see their problem clearly.

## How to use this

- Film vertical (9:16). Phone is fine. Good light on your face, clean audio beats everything.
- Open with the hook in the first 2 seconds. No "Hi, I'm Tal." Earn the intro.
- One idea per video. Do not cram.
- Burn captions in. Most people watch muted.
- End screen or pinned comment: "Sales stuck? Tell the Sales Doctor at talpaperin.com." or
  "Book a 15 minute call, link in bio/comment." Keep the link in the first comment on LinkedIn,
  it suppresses reach in the body.
- Post 3 to 5 a week. Reuse the same scripts across all four platforms.
- No em dashes, no hashtag salad, no fake urgency. Your edge is that you sound like an operator,
  not a content creator.

Each script below: a HOOK (say it to camera, first line), the BODY (the point), and the
CLOSE (the line that sends them somewhere). Run times are rough at a normal speaking pace.

## 2. Pipeline looks full, nothing closes (about 45s)
Source: Sales Doctor / case study teasers

HOOK: A full pipeline is not a good sign. Sometimes it is the most dangerous number on your dashboard.

BODY: Reps love a fat pipeline because it looks like work. But if deals sit in stages with no exit criteria, your pipeline is a junk drawer. Half those deals already said no, they just have not told you yet. A real pipeline has dates, next steps, and a reason the buyer moves this quarter. If you cannot name that reason, it is not a deal. It is a hope.

## 3. The founder is the only closer (about 40s)
Source: homepage "What I Fix"

The hard truth is your reps cannot close because you never built the thing they would sell with: the story, the qualification, the objection answers that live outside your head. ## 5. Demos go great, then silence (about 40s)
Source: Sales Doctor example prompts

HOOK: The demo went great and then they ghosted you. That is not a follow-up problem. It is a deal you never actually had.

BODY: A great demo means they liked the product. It says nothing about budget, authority, or whether anyone internally is willing to fight for this. You sold the feature. Nobody sold the change. If you did not surface who loses if this does not happen, the deal dies the moment the room goes quiet. Excitement is not a buying signal. Consequence is.

## 7. Discounting is a symptom (about 35s)
Source: pricing blog cluster

HOOK: If your team only closes when they discount, you do not have a pricing problem. You have a value problem.

BODY: A discount is what a rep reaches for when they cannot defend the price. The fix is not a tighter discount policy. It is teaching the team to anchor on the cost of the buyer doing nothing. When the prospect feels what the problem is costing them every month, your price stops being the conversation. Discounting is the sound of a sales team that lost the value argument early.

## 8. Hiring a fractional CRO vs a VP (about 45s)
Source: fractional-cro-vs-vp-of-sales page

HOOK: Founders ask me whether they should hire a VP of Sales or a fractional CRO. Wrong question. Ask what stage your sales actually is.

BODY: If you have a proven motion and you just need someone to scale a team, hire the VP. If you do not yet know why deals close, what your real ICP is, or whether the motion even works, a full-time VP is an expensive way to find out. That is the stage where you bring in someone who has built it before, part time, to design the machine. Then you hire the VP to run it. Hire to the stage, not to the title.

## 11. Distributors are not a sales strategy (about 40s)
Source: Distributor and Channel Partner Recruitment page

HOOK: "We will just sign distributors" is the most expensive shortcut in B2B. I have watched it sink good companies.

BODY: A distributor does not create demand. They fulfill it. If your product does not already pull, you have just handed your brand to someone who carries forty other lines and will push whatever is easiest that month. Channel works when you have proven the sale yourself and you are scaling reach. It does not work as a way to avoid learning how to sell. Sign partners from strength, not from desperation.

## 12. The dashboard is lying to you (about 35s)
Source: general POV

HOOK: Your sales dashboard looks fine and your bank account disagrees. One of them is lying, and it is the dashboard.

BODY: Activity metrics make everyone feel busy. Calls made, demos booked, pipeline created. None of that is revenue. The numbers that matter are boring: win rate by stage, cycle length, and how many deals are real versus parked. When leaders manage the vanity metrics, the team optimizes for looking productive instead of closing. Measure the few things that move cash, ignore the rest.

Source: homepage "What I Fix"

HOOK: Your reps improvise every sales call because you never gave them anything to follow. Then you call it a talent problem.

BODY: Walk into most B2B teams and ask three reps how they qualify a deal. You will get three different answers, and two of them are guesses. That is not because the reps are bad. It is because no one built the playbook: the questions, the qualification, the answers to the five objections you hear every week. Without it, every call is a coin flip and your forecast is fiction. The fix is not motivation. It is a system.

## 15. The "we need more leads" trap (about 40s)
Source: b2b-lead-generation page / POV

HOOK: When a founder tells me "we just need more leads," I already know leads are not the problem.

BODY: More leads into a broken funnel just means more deals dying in the same place. If you close two out of ten today, doubling leads gets you more no, faster, and a burned-out team. Fix the conversion first. Find the stage where deals go to die, fix that one thing, and your existing leads suddenly produce more revenue with zero extra spend. Volume hides problems. It never solves them.
